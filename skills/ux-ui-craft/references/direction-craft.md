# Direction craft — deriving a look that could only belong to this subject

Read at Step 2, when the direction is being chosen. The mean is a gravity
well; "pick a direction" without a method slides back into it. A direction
is an argument about the subject, not a mood.

## The derivation ladder

1. **Mine the subject's world before touching aesthetics.** List the
   physical materials, tools, and *printed matter* the trade actually
   lives with: a bookkeeping API lives in ledgers, journal columns,
   hairline rules; a clinic in appointment slips, queue tickets, chart
   folders; a warehouse in dock boards, manifests, thermal labels; a
   coffee roaster in kraft bags, batch tags, cupping sheets. This list is
   the raw ore — ten minutes here buys more distinctiveness than any
   amount of palette tweaking later.
2. **Pick ONE material metaphor as the spine.** Not three. The metaphor
   decides paper color, type voice, rule weight, and texture in one
   stroke — that's why it works: coherence comes free.
3. **Position against the category default.** Write down what the lazy
   answer looks like for this brief ("light SaaS, blue accent, rounded
   cards"; "dark AI tool, glow accent"). Your direction must differ from
   it *structurally* — in layout bones, type construction, or material —
   not just in hue. A re-colored default is still the default.
4. **Name it as a compound.** Two or three words that pair the trade with
   a register: *retro-terminal workshop · warm-clinical calm · dock
   control desk · ledger-precise technical · order-ticket kitchen*. If
   the name could caption a different industry's site, keep digging.
5. **Derive the tokens FROM the metaphor** — paper from its material,
   accent from its marking color (stamp red, soldermask green, safety
   amber), type from its print tradition. Tokens chosen this way defend
   themselves; tokens chosen by taste alone drift under pressure.

## The domain-artifact move (highest yield per effort)

Take the trade's own artifact — the slip, ticket, ledger row, spec plate,
boarding card, dock board — and make a *working replica* of it the
interface's signature element. Let it do real UI work (the appointment
slip fills as the form fills; the journal entry actually balances; the
ticket accumulates the order). It lands because reviewers recognize it in
one second, it proves domain fluency, and it gives the design one honest
piece of theater while everything around it stays quiet.

*Test: would a practitioner of this trade recognize the artifact without
a label? Would they spot an error in it?* (If your fake ledger doesn't
balance, they will — and trust dies there.)

## Insider fluency — details that prove you know the trade

Distinctiveness is mostly *specificity*: real units (dp, ctn, m², lô),
the trade's own vocabulary in labels, plausible identifiers (HĐ 0000461,
INB-90422), workflows in the right order, prices with the right
formatting. One paragraph of fluent domain copy outworks any gradient.
This is the same rule as "real copy everywhere," aimed at expertise.

## Boldness budget, spent precisely

- One signature; place it where the eye lands first (hero) or let it
  recur *with meaning* (the same ticket evolving across a flow — cart →
  paid receipt). Recurrence without evolution is wallpaper.
- Everything within 100px of the signature stays quiet — the signature
  needs negative space to read as intentional.

## Direction smoke tests (run before Step 3)

1. **Default test** — would any similar brief produce this? (Existing.)
2. **Caption test** — does the direction's name only fit this subject?
3. **Grayscale test** — strip the color mentally: does the direction
   survive on structure and type alone? If it only exists as a palette,
   it's a theme, not a direction.
4. **Practitioner test** — name three details an insider would notice
   and nod at. Can't name three → mine the world again (step 1).
